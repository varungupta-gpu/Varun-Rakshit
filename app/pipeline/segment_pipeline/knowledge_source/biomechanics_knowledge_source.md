# Gather Phase

## Balanced Gather

### Definition

A Balanced Gather is characterized by stable head control, consistent body alignment, and controlled loading throughout the gather phase. The bowler maintains an organized posture with minimal instability, allowing movement to remain coordinated and mechanically efficient.

### Identification Criteria
- Head Stability: High
- Head Position Variability: Low
- Hip-Shoulder Alignment Variability: Low
- Loading Variability: Low to Moderate
- Corrective Movements: Minimal

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Balanced if : 

- Head movement is highly variable
- Alignment frequently breaks down
- Loading patterns are erratic
- Repeated corrective actions are present
- Strong instability is observed

## Smooth Gather

### Definition

A Smooth Gather is characterized by gradual, continuous, and coordinated movement progression throughout the gather phase. The bowler transitions between positions with minimal abrupt corrections, allowing rhythm and momentum to build naturally.

### Identification Criteria
- Smoothness Score: High
- Tilt Variability: Low to Moderate
- Movement Progression: Gradual
- Directional Reversals: Minimal

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Smooth if:

- Shoulder tilt variability is high
- Movement contains frequent abrupt corrections
- Motion appears jerky or inconsistent

## Shoulder-Led Gather

### Definition

A Shoulder-Led Gather is characterized by the shoulders playing the dominant role in shaping movement during the gather phase. Changes in shoulder tilt and upper-body positioning contribute more strongly to the gather pattern than hip loading or lower-body movement.

### Identification Criteria
- Shoulder Tilt Variability: Moderate to High
- Shoulder Tilt Progression: Clearly Observable
- Hip Loading Magnitude: Low to Moderate
- Hip-Shoulder Alignment: Stable or Controlled

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Shoulder-Led if:

- Hip loading dominates the gather phase
- Shoulder movement is minimal
- Lower-body movement drives most positional changes
- Shoulder tilt progression is limited or absent

## Hip-Led Gather

### Definition

A Hip-Led Gather is characterized by the hips acting as the primary driver of movement during the gather phase. Hip loading and lower-body positioning contribute more strongly to the gather pattern than shoulder-driven movement, helping establish balance, rhythm, and body organization.

### Identification Criteria
- Hip Loading Magnitude: Moderate to High
- Hip Loading Variability: Controlled
- Shoulder Tilt Variability: Low to Moderate
- Hip-Shoulder Alignment: Stable or Controlled

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Hip-Led if:

- Shoulder movement dominates the gather phase
- Hip loading is minimal
- Upper-body mechanics drive most positional changes
- Shoulder tilt progression is substantially greater than hip movement

## Slight Movement, Organized Gather

### Definition

A Slight Movement, Organized Gather is characterized by controlled and purposeful movement during the gather phase without excessive body displacement. The bowler remains well-organized while using small to moderate movements to establish rhythm and prepare for the delivery stride.

### Identification Criteria
- Hip Loading Magnitude: Low to Moderate
- Head Stability: High
- Hip-Shoulder Alignment Variability: Low
- Movement Variability: Controlled
- Instability: Minimal

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Slight Movement, Organized if:

- Movement is excessively large or aggressive
- Significant instability is present
- Alignment frequently breaks down
- Body positioning appears erratic or uncoordinated
- Gather remains completely static with minimal movement

## Left-Side Loaded Gather

### Definition

A Left-Side Loaded Gather is characterized by a greater proportion of body loading occurring on the left side during the gather phase. The bowler naturally shifts or maintains more load through the left side, creating a noticeable left-biased loading pattern.

### Identification Criteria
- Dominant Side: Left
- Left Load Percentage > Right Load Percentage
- Consistent left-side loading tendency throughout the gather
- Loading asymmetry favors the left side

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Left-Side Loaded if:

- Right-side loading is dominant
- Loading remains largely symmetrical
- No consistent left-side preference is observed
- Loading direction frequently alternates between sides

## Right-Side Loaded Gather

### Definition

A Right-Side Loaded Gather is characterized by a greater proportion of body loading occurring on the right side during the gather phase. The bowler naturally shifts or maintains more load through the right side, creating a noticeable right-biased loading pattern.

### Identification Criteria
- Dominant Side: Right
- Right Load Percentage > Left Load Percentage
- Consistent right-side loading tendency throughout the gather
- Loading asymmetry favors the right side

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Right-Side Loaded if:

- Left-side loading is dominant
- Loading remains largely symmetrical
- No consistent right-side preference is observed
- Loading direction frequently alternates between sides

## Unstable Gather

### Definition

An Unstable Gather is characterized by inconsistent body control and frequent movement variability during the gather phase. The bowler struggles to maintain stable positioning, resulting in repeated adjustments, alignment changes, or loss of movement consistency.

### Identification Criteria
- Head Stability: Low
- Head Position Variability: High
- Hip-Shoulder Alignment Variability: High
- Hip Loading Variability: High
- Corrective Movements: Frequent

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Unstable if:

- Head position remains stable
- Alignment is consistently maintained
- Loading patterns are controlled
- Corrective movements are minimal
- Movement remains organized throughout the gather phase

## Oscillatory Gather

### Definition

An Oscillatory Gather is characterized by repeated back-and-forth movement patterns during the gather phase. Rather than progressing in a consistently directed manner, the bowler exhibits recurring directional changes that create a rhythmic oscillating motion.

### Identification Criteria
- Frequent directional reversals
- Repeated changes in movement direction
- Wave-like movement progression
- Oscillatory patterns in shoulder tilt or loading behaviour

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Oscillatory if:

- Movement progresses consistently in one direction
- Directional reversals are minimal
- Motion remains smooth and continuous
- No recurring back-and-forth pattern is observed

## Compact Gather

### Definition

A Compact Gather is characterized by movement occurring within a relatively small range during the gather phase. The bowler maintains a contained and efficient loading pattern, with limited body displacement while preparing for the delivery stride.

### Identification Criteria
- Movement Magnitude: Low
- Maximum Displacement: Low
- Hip Loading Excursion: Limited
- Hip-Shoulder Alignment Changes: Small
- Overall Movement Range: Compact

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Compact if:

- Movement amplitudes are large
- Hip loading excursions are substantial
- Alignment changes are pronounced
- Body displacement is excessive
- The gather occupies a large movement range

# Backfoot phase

## Side-On Backfoot Phase

### Definition

A Side-On Backfoot Phase is characterized by the bowler maintaining a side-oriented body position at backfoot contact. The hips and trunk are aligned predominantly sideways relative to the target, creating a classic side-on loading posture.

### Identification Criteria
- Stance Orientation: Side-On
- Average Stance Angle: Low
- Body Alignment: Predominantly Sideways
- Front-On Characteristics: Minimal

#3# Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Side-On if:

- The stance is predominantly front-on
- Average stance angle is high
- Body orientation faces the target directly
- Semi side-on or mixed-action characteristics dominate

## Semi Side-On Backfoot Phase

### Definition

A Semi Side-On Backfoot Phase is characterized by a partially side-oriented body position at backfoot contact. The bowler maintains a balanced blend of side-on and front-on characteristics, resulting in an intermediate stance orientation.

### Identification Criteria
- Stance Orientation: Semi Side-On
- Average Stance Angle: Moderate
- Body Alignment: Partially Sideways
- Side-On and Front-On Characteristics: Balanced

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Semi Side-On if:

- The stance is predominantly side-on
- The stance is predominantly front-on
- Average stance angle falls clearly within side-on or front-on ranges
- Body orientation strongly favors one alignment style

## Front-On Backfoot Phase :

### Definition

A Front-On Backfoot Phase is characterized by the bowler adopting a forward-facing body position at backfoot contact. The hips and trunk are oriented more directly towards the target, resulting in a predominantly front-on loading posture.

### Identification Criteria
- Stance Orientation: Front-On
- Average Stance Angle: High
- Body Alignment: Predominantly Forward-Facing
- Front-On Characteristics: Dominant

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Front-On if:

- The stance is predominantly side-on
- Average stance angle is low
- Body orientation remains largely sideways
- Semi side-on characteristics dominate

## Deep Compression Back-Leg

### Definition

A Deep Compression Back-Leg is characterized by substantial flexion of the back knee during the backfoot phase. The bowler loads deeply into the back leg, creating a compressed lower-body position that can assist with force generation and momentum transfer into the delivery stride.

### Identification Criteria
- Back Knee Angle: Low
- Back Knee Flexion: High
- Back-Leg Loading: Pronounced
- Knee Compression: Significant

## Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Deep Compression Back-Leg if:

- Back knee angle remains relatively high
- Knee flexion is limited
- Back-leg loading is minimal
- The back leg remains predominantly extended
- Compression is not a defining feature of the posture

## Extended Back-Leg

### Definition

An Extended Back-Leg is characterized by the back knee remaining relatively straight during the backfoot phase. The bowler utilizes a more extended lower-body posture with limited knee compression while loading into the delivery stride.

### Identification Criteria
- Back Knee Angle: High
- Back Knee Flexion: Low
- Back-Leg Compression: Limited
- Knee Extension: Pronounced

## Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Extended Back-Leg if:

- Back knee angle is low
- Deep knee flexion is present
- Significant back-leg compression occurs
- The lower body adopts a strongly compressed posture
- Knee extension is not a defining feature of the movement

## Hip-Dominant Backfoot Phase

### Definition

A Hip-Dominant Backfoot Phase is characterized by the hips serving as the primary driver of loading and body organization during backfoot contact. Hip positioning, elevation, and loading patterns play a more prominent role than other movement features in shaping the bowler's posture.

### Identification Criteria
- Hip Loading Magnitude: Moderate to High
- Hip Loading Consistency: High
- Dominant Side Loading: Clearly Present
- Hip Variability: Low to Moderate

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Hip-Dominant if:

- Hip loading is minimal
- No clear loading side is present
- Hip positioning varies substantially
- Knee mechanics or stance orientation dominate the posture
- Hip contribution is not a defining feature of the movement

## Progressive Smooth Backfoot Phase

### Definition

A Progressive Smooth Backfoot Phase is characterized by gradual and continuous movement progression throughout backfoot contact. The bowler transitions between positions in a coordinated manner, with minimal abrupt changes in loading, knee position, or body organization.

### Identification Criteria
- Movement Progression: Gradual
- Position Changes: Continuous
- Knee Angle Progression: Consistent
- Abrupt Corrections: Minimal

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Progressive Smooth if:

- Position changes occur abruptly
- Loading patterns fluctuate irregularly
- Frequent corrective movements are present
- Knee-angle progression is inconsistent
- Movement lacks continuity between frames

## Variable Backfoot Phase

### Definition

A Variable Backfoot Phase is characterized by noticeable fluctuations in movement, loading, or joint positioning during backfoot contact. The bowler exhibits changing movement patterns rather than maintaining a highly consistent progression throughout the phase.

### Identification Criteria
- Movement Variability: High
- Knee Angle Variability: High
- Loading Consistency: Reduced
- Position Changes: Inconsistent

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Variable if:

- Movement progression is smooth and repeatable
- Knee-angle changes are highly consistent
- Loading patterns remain stable
- Position changes follow a predictable progression
- Variability is minimal throughout the phase

## Mixed-Action Backfoot Phase

### Definition

A Mixed-Action Backfoot Phase is characterized by the presence of multiple competing movement characteristics rather than a single dominant loading or positioning strategy. The bowler exhibits a blend of stance, loading, and movement patterns that do not fit clearly into one biomechanical category.

### Identification Criteria
- No single movement characteristic is strongly dominant
- Features from multiple posture categories are present
- Loading and positioning patterns show mixed characteristics
- Classification remains ambiguous between distinct styles

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Mixed-Action if:

- A single posture classification is clearly dominant
- Stance orientation is strongly defined
- Loading characteristics consistently favor one movement strategy
- Movement patterns fit clearly within an existing category

# Delivery stride phase

## Straight-Line Delivery Stride

### Definition

A Straight-Line Delivery Stride is characterized by the bowler moving directly towards front-foot contact along a consistent and linear stride path. The delivery stride remains aligned with the intended direction of movement, with minimal lateral deviation.

### Identification Criteria
- Stride Direction Angle: Near the intended movement line
- Lateral Deviation: Minimal
- Stride Path Consistency: High
- Across-Body Movement: Limited

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Straight-Line if:

- The stride path deviates substantially from the intended line
- Significant cross-body movement is present
- Lateral movement dominates the stride
- Stride direction changes noticeably during the phase

## Balanced Delivery Stride

### Definition

A Balanced Delivery Stride is characterized by controlled body positioning, stable alignment, and coordinated movement throughout the delivery stride. The bowler maintains postural control while transferring momentum towards front-foot contact, with minimal instability or unnecessary compensatory actions.

### Identification Criteria
- Head Stability: High
- Alignment Variability: Low to Moderate
- Trunk Control: Stable
- Stride Consistency: High
- Instability: Minimal

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Balanced if:

- Head movement is highly unstable
- Alignment changes are excessive
- Trunk movement is erratic
- Repeated compensatory actions occur
- Instability dominates the delivery stride

## Head-Controlled Delivery Stride

### Definition

A Head-Controlled Delivery Stride is characterized by the bowler maintaining a stable and controlled head position throughout the delivery stride. The head serves as the primary reference point for body organization, helping coordinate alignment, balance, and movement into front-foot contact.

### Identification Criteria
- Head Stability: High
- Head Position Variability: Low
- Head Movement Control: Consistent
- Postural Organization Around the Head: Evident

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Head-Controlled if:

- Head movement is highly variable
- Head stability is poor
- Frequent head corrections occur
- Body organization is not centered around head control
- Instability dominates the stride

## Trunk-Driven Delivery Stride

### Definition

A Trunk-Driven Delivery Stride is characterized by the trunk serving as the primary driver of movement organization during the delivery stride. Changes in trunk position and lateral flexion contribute substantially to body progression, momentum transfer, and delivery mechanics.

### Identification Criteria
- Trunk Lateral Flexion Magnitude: Moderate to High
- Trunk Movement Contribution: Dominant
- Trunk Progression: Clearly Observable
- Body Organization: Influenced primarily by trunk movement

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Trunk-Driven if:

- Trunk movement is minimal
- Head control is the dominant characteristic
- Stride direction is the primary defining feature
- Trunk contribution is secondary to other movement patterns
- Little change in trunk position occurs during the delivery stride

## Across-Body Delivery Stride

### Definition

An Across-Body Delivery Stride is characterized by the bowler's stride path moving across the body's intended line of progression during the delivery stride. Rather than advancing directly towards the target, the stride exhibits a noticeable cross-body component.

### Identification Criteria
- Stride Direction Deviation: High
- Cross-Body Movement: Clearly Present
- Stride Path Alignment: Deviates from the intended line
- Lateral Stride Component: Pronounced

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Across-Body if:

- The stride path remains predominantly linear
- Lateral deviation is minimal
- Foot placement follows the intended movement line
- Straight-line progression is maintained throughout the stride

## Open Delivery Stride

### Definition

An Open Delivery Stride is characterized by the hips and shoulders adopting an open alignment during the delivery stride. The upper body is oriented more towards the target rather than remaining closed or aligned along the line of movement.

### Identification Criteria
- Hip-Shoulder Alignment Magnitude: High
- Open Alignment Characteristics: Present
- Upper-Body Orientation: Towards the Target
- Closed Alignment Characteristics: Limited

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Open if:

- Hip-shoulder alignment remains low
- Body positioning stays predominantly closed
- The shoulders remain aligned along the movement line
- Open-body characteristics are minimal

## Upper-Body Dominant Delivery Stride

### Definition

An Upper-Body Dominant Delivery Stride is characterized by the head, trunk, and upper-body alignment contributing more strongly to the movement pattern than stride-path mechanics. The defining features of the delivery stride are primarily expressed through upper-body positioning and motion.

### Identification Criteria
- Upper-Body Movement Signatures: Prominent
- Head Contribution: Significant
- Trunk Contribution: Significant
- Hip-Shoulder Alignment Influence: High
- Upper-Body Features: More prominent than stride-path features

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Upper-Body Dominant if:

- Upper-body movement is minimal
- Stride direction is the defining feature of the action
- Head and trunk contributions are secondary
- Movement characteristics are driven primarily by lower-body mechanics
- Upper-body signatures are not prominent

## Side-Leaning Delivery Stride

### Definition

A Side-Leaning Delivery Stride is characterized by a consistent lateral inclination of the head and/or trunk towards one side during the delivery stride. The bowler exhibits a clear directional lean bias rather than maintaining a vertically centered posture.

### Identification Criteria
- Lateral Lean Magnitude: Moderate to High
- Dominant Lean Direction: Clearly Present
- Head or Trunk Bias: Consistent
- Side Inclination: Observable throughout the stride

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Side-Leaning if:

- Head and trunk remain centrally aligned
- Lean direction changes frequently
- Lateral inclination is minimal
- No clear side bias is present
- Body posture remains predominantly upright

# Frontfoot phase

## Strong Braced Frontfoot Phase

### Definition

A Strong Braced Frontfoot Phase is characterized by the front leg remaining close to full extension at front-foot contact and release. The front knee acts as a rigid support structure, resisting collapse and providing a stable base for force transfer through the body.

### Identification Criteria
- Front Knee Angle: High
- Front Knee Extension: Near Full
- Knee Collapse: Minimal
- Front-Leg Support: Strong

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Strong Braced if:

- The front knee remains noticeably flexed
- Significant knee collapse occurs after front-foot contact
- Front-leg support is inconsistent
- Force absorption dominates over force blocking
- Front-leg extension is limited

## High Release Arm Frontfoot Phase

### Definition

A High Release Arm Frontfoot Phase is characterized by the bowling arm maintaining a highly extended position approaching ball release. The release action is associated with an elevated arm path and a strongly extended bowling-arm configuration.

### Identification Criteria
- Bowling Arm Extension: High
- Elbow Flexion: Minimal
- Release Arm Position: Elevated
- Arm Alignment at Release: Near Full Extension

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as High Release Arm if:

- Significant elbow flexion is present at release
- The bowling arm remains noticeably bent
- Arm extension is inconsistent
- Release occurs with a compact arm position
- Elevated release-arm characteristics are absent

## Trunk-Driven Frontfoot Phase

### Definition

A Trunk-Driven Frontfoot Phase is characterized by substantial trunk involvement during the release action. The trunk serves as a major contributor to momentum transfer and release mechanics through pronounced lateral flexion and active body movement.

### Identification Criteria
- Trunk Lateral Flexion Magnitude: High
- Trunk Contribution: Dominant
- Trunk Movement Progression: Clearly Present
- Release Mechanics: Influenced by Trunk Action

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Trunk-Driven if:

- Trunk movement is minimal
- Release mechanics are dominated by arm action
- Trunk contribution is secondary to other movement patterns
- Little change in trunk position occurs approaching release
- Lateral trunk flexion remains consistently low

## Braced Power Release

### Definition

A Braced Power Release is characterized by the combination of a strongly braced front leg and an efficient release mechanism. The bowler utilizes front-leg blocking to create a stable base from which momentum can be transferred into the bowling arm and release action.

### Identification Criteria
- Front-Leg Bracing: High
- Front Knee Extension: Near Full
- Release Arm Extension: High
- Momentum Transfer Through Release: Evident

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Braced Power Release if:

- Front-leg bracing is absent
- Significant front-knee collapse occurs
- Bowling-arm extension is limited
- Release mechanics are not supported by a stable front-leg block
- Momentum transfer through the front leg is minimal

## Controlled Release

### Definition

A Controlled Release is characterized by consistent organization of body segments during the frontfoot and release phase. The bowler maintains coordinated positioning of the front leg, trunk, alignment, and bowling arm, resulting in a repeatable and well-organized release action.

### Identification Criteria
- Alignment Consistency: High
- Release Position Variability: Low
- Front-Leg Position: Stable
- Bowling Arm Configuration: Consistent
- Segment Coordination: Well Organized

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Controlled Release if:

- Release positions vary substantially
- Alignment changes excessively approaching release
- Front-leg mechanics are inconsistent
- Bowling-arm configuration changes unpredictably
- Release mechanics appear highly variable

## Flexible Front-Leg

### Definition

A Flexible Front-Leg is characterized by the front knee remaining noticeably flexed during the frontfoot phase rather than forming a rigid brace. The front leg absorbs and manages load through knee flexion, creating a softer support structure at release.

### Identification Criteria
- Front Knee Angle: Moderate to Low
- Front Knee Flexion: Present
- Front-Leg Compliance: High
- Knee Collapse Resistance: Reduced

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Flexible Front-Leg if:

- The front knee remains near full extension
- A strong front-leg brace is present
- Knee collapse is minimal
- The front leg acts as a rigid blocking structure
- Front-leg extension dominates the release mechanics

## Arm-Dominant Release

### Definition

An Arm-Dominant Release is characterized by the bowling arm being the most prominent feature of the release action. The release mechanics are expressed primarily through bowling-arm position and extension rather than through pronounced front-leg bracing, trunk-driven motion, or alignment-based mechanics.

### Identification Criteria
- Bowling Arm Extension: High
- Bowling Arm Contribution: Prominent
- Elbow Flexion: Minimal
- Release Characteristics: Primarily Arm-Led

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Arm-Dominant Release if:

- Trunk movement is the primary defining feature
- Front-leg bracing dominates the release pattern
- Alignment mechanics are more prominent than arm mechanics
- Bowling-arm extension is limited
- Arm contribution is not a defining characteristic of the release

## Alignment-Controlled Release

### Definition

An Alignment-Controlled Release is characterized by hip–shoulder alignment serving as the primary organizing feature of the release action. The bowler maintains a consistent and structured body alignment approaching release, allowing the delivery mechanics to be governed by alignment rather than by isolated segment actions.

### Identification Criteria
- Hip-Shoulder Alignment Magnitude: Clearly Defined
- Alignment Consistency: High
- Alignment Variability: Low to Moderate
- Release Organization: Alignment-Led

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Alignment-Controlled if:

- Hip–shoulder alignment varies substantially
- Alignment breaks down approaching release
- Trunk movement dominates the release mechanics
- Bowling-arm action is the defining feature of the release
- No clear alignment pattern is maintained

## Dynamic Release

### Definition

A Dynamic Release is characterized by substantial body movement and active segment progression during the frontfoot phase. The release action is marked by pronounced mechanical activity, with body segments continuing to move and reposition as the ball is delivered.

### Identification Criteria
- Segment Movement Magnitude: High
- Trunk Movement: Pronounced
- Mechanical Activity: High
- Release Progression: Active and Continuous
- Positional Change During Release: Evident

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Dynamic Release if:

- Body segments remain relatively static during release
- Trunk movement is minimal
- Release is dominated by positional stability rather than movement
- Segment progression is limited
- Mechanical activity remains consistently low

# follow through phase

## Complete Follow Through

### Definition

A Complete Follow Through is characterized by the bowling action continuing naturally after ball release, allowing momentum to dissipate through the entire kinetic chain. The bowler does not abruptly stop or restrict movement after release and completes the delivery action through to its natural conclusion.

### Identification Criteria
- Follow Through Completion: High
- Post-Release Movement Continuity: Present
- Momentum Dissipation: Natural
- Movement Interruption: Minimal

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Complete Follow Through if:

- Follow-through is prematurely terminated
- Movement stops abruptly after release
- Wrist or arm motion is restricted immediately after release
- Momentum dissipation appears incomplete
- Post-release action is noticeably shortened

## Controlled Follow Through

### Definition

A Controlled Follow Through is characterized by the bowler maintaining balance, stability, and body control after ball release. The follow-through remains coordinated and organized, with minimal loss of posture or uncontrolled movement.

### Identification Criteria

- Post-Delivery Balance: High
- Body Control: Consistent
- Postural Stability: Maintained
- Uncontrolled Movement: Minimal

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Controlled Follow Through if:

- Balance is frequently lost after release
- Significant postural breakdown occurs
- Uncontrolled body movement is evident
- Recovery after delivery is unstable
- Large numbers of unbalanced frames are present

## Full Extension Follow Through

### Definition

A Full Extension Follow Through is characterized by the bowler allowing the release-side body segments to reach near-complete extension before the follow-through concludes. The bowling action finishes with an elongated and fully expressed movement pattern rather than an abbreviated or compact finish.

### Identification Criteria
- Segment Extension: High
- Post-Release Reach: Full
- Movement Expression: Complete
- Follow-Through Length: Extended

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Full Extension Follow Through if:

- Follow-through ends prematurely
- Segment motion is noticeably shortened
- Post-release reach is limited
- Movement appears compact or abbreviated
- Follow-through extension is restricted

## Upright Follow Through

### Definition

An Upright Follow Through is characterized by the bowler maintaining a relatively vertical body posture after ball release. The trunk remains close to neutral alignment, minimizing excessive lateral lean and preserving an upright finishing position.

### Identification Criteria
- Trunk Lateral Flexion Magnitude: Low
- Postural Deviation from Vertical: Minimal
- Upright Body Position: Maintained
- Side Lean: Limited

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Upright Follow Through if:

- Significant trunk side-flexion occurs
- Pronounced body lean is evident
- The trunk departs substantially from vertical alignment
- Follow-through posture collapses laterally
- Side-bending dominates the finishing action

## Trunk-Driven Follow Through

### Definition

A Trunk-Driven Follow Through is characterized by substantial trunk involvement during the finishing phase of the bowling action. The trunk continues to contribute actively after release through pronounced lateral flexion and body movement, becoming the dominant feature of the follow-through pattern.

### Identification Criteria
- Trunk Lateral Flexion Magnitude: High
- Trunk Contribution: Dominant
- Trunk Movement Continuation: Evident
- Follow-Through Mechanics: Trunk-Led

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Trunk-Driven Follow Through if:

- Trunk movement is minimal
- Follow-through occurs with little trunk involvement
- Postural changes remain small throughout the finish
- Trunk action is secondary to other movement patterns
- Lateral flexion remains consistently low

## Restricted Follow Through

### Definition

A Restricted Follow Through is characterized by a shortened or prematurely terminated finishing action after ball release. The bowler does not allow the delivery motion to progress through its full natural range, resulting in a limited follow-through pattern.

### Identification Criteria
- Follow-Through Completion: Reduced
- Post-Release Motion: Limited
- Movement Continuation: Restricted
- Follow-Through Length: Shortened

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Restricted Follow Through if:

- Follow-through continues naturally after release
- Movement reaches a complete finishing action
- Post-release motion remains uninterrupted
- Momentum is allowed to dissipate fully
- Follow-through displays full extension and completion

# Head position and stability 

## Centered Head

### Definition

A Centered Head is characterized by the head remaining close to a neutral position throughout the movement phase. No meaningful leftward or rightward positional bias is consistently present.

### Identification Criteria
- Head Position Bias: Minimal
- Signed Head Position Mean: Near Zero
- Lateral Head Deviation: Low
- Positional Symmetry: Present

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Centered Head if:

- Consistent leftward bias exists
- Consistent rightward bias exists
- Strong lateral leaning is observed
- Head position remains asymmetrical throughout the phase

## Stable Head

### Definition

A Stable Head is characterized by minimal variation in head position throughout the movement phase. The head remains consistently controlled with limited unnecessary movement or positional fluctuation.

### Identification Criteria
- Head Position Variability: Low
- Head Stability Score: High
- Positional Consistency: High
- Unnecessary Head Movement: Minimal

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Stable Head if:

- Head position changes substantially during the phase
- Frequent head corrections occur
- Positional variability is high
- Head movement appears inconsistent
- Large fluctuations in head position are present

## Left-Side Head Bias

### Definition

A Left-Side Head Bias is characterized by the head consistently occupying a position left of the neutral midline throughout the movement phase. The head demonstrates a clear directional preference toward the left side, although the magnitude of the deviation may vary.

### Identification Criteria
- Head Position Bias: Leftward
- Signed Head Position Mean: Left-Oriented
- Dominant Head Position: Left of Center
- Directional Preference: Consistent

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Left-Side Head Bias if:

- Head position remains centered overall
- Rightward bias is dominant
- No consistent directional preference exists
- Head oscillates equally around the center line

## Right-Side Head Bias

### Definition

A Right-Side Head Bias is characterized by the head consistently occupying a position right of the neutral midline throughout the movement phase. The head demonstrates a clear directional preference toward the right side, although the magnitude of the deviation may vary.

### Identification Criteria
- Head Position Bias: Rightward
- Signed Head Position Mean: Right-Oriented
- Dominant Head Position: Right of Center
- Directional Preference: Consistent

### Exclusions

# bowling arm mechanics 

## Complete Release

### Definition

A Complete Release is characterized by the bowling arm moving through its full release pathway with minimal restriction. The arm extends efficiently through ball release and continues naturally into the early follow-through, indicating uninterrupted energy transfer and mechanical completion of the bowling action.

### Identification Criteria
- Bowling Arm Extension: High
- Release Arm Position: Fully Extended
- Release Completion: Full
- Follow-Through Continuity: Maintained
- Mechanical Restriction: Minimal

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Complete Release if:

- The bowling arm decelerates prematurely
- Arm extension remains incomplete
- Release action appears shortened
- Follow-through is heavily restricted
- Mechanical interruption occurs before release completion

## Strong Release & Finish

### Definition

A Strong Release & Finish is characterized by an effective ball release followed by a pronounced continuation of arm movement through the follow-through. The bowling arm not only achieves a complete release position but also maintains momentum beyond release, producing a powerful and committed finishing action.

### Identification Criteria
- Release Arm Extension: High
- Follow-Through Completion: High
- Arm Deceleration: Gradual
- Post-Release Arm Travel: Extensive
- Movement Commitment: Strong

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Strong Release & Finish if:

- Follow-through is noticeably restricted
- Arm motion stops shortly after release
- Release is complete but finishing action is limited
- Arm decelerates abruptly
- Post-release movement is minimal

## Extended Release, Limited Finish

### Definition

An Extended Release, Limited Finish is characterized by a well-extended bowling arm at ball release, followed by a relatively restricted or shortened follow-through. The bowler achieves an effective release position but does not carry the arm through a full finishing action.

### Identification Criteria
- Release Arm Extension: High
- Elbow Extension at Release: High
- Follow-Through Completion: Low to Moderate
- Post-Release Arm Travel: Limited
- Arm Deceleration: Early

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Extended Release, Limited Finish if:

- Follow-through continues extensively after release
- Arm momentum is carried fully into the finish
- Post-release arm travel remains large
- Finishing action is a dominant feature
- Arm deceleration occurs gradually over a long range

## Compact Release

### Definition

A Compact Release is characterized by a controlled and efficient arm action in which the bowling arm achieves ball release with relatively limited movement amplitude. The release is mechanically economical, emphasizing precision and efficiency rather than large-range arm motion.

### Identification Criteria
- Release Arm Range: Compact
- Arm Path Length: Short to Moderate
- Elbow Extension: Functional but not exaggerated
- Movement Economy: High
- Release Efficiency: High

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Compact Release if:

- Release involves a large arm arc
- Arm extension is exaggerated
- Follow-through dominates the action
- Arm travel continues extensively after release
- Release mechanics emphasize range over efficiency

## Follow-Through Dominant

### Definition

A Follow-Through Dominant action is characterized by the bowling arm continuing well beyond ball release, with the finishing phase becoming the most prominent feature of the arm mechanics. The emphasis is placed on momentum continuation rather than the release position itself.

### Identification Criteria

- Follow-Through Range: High
- Post-Release Arm Travel: Extensive
- Momentum Continuation: High
- Arm Deceleration: Gradual
- Finishing Action Contribution: Dominant

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Follow-Through Dominant if:

- Arm motion stops soon after release
- Follow-through is restricted
- Release position is the primary defining feature
- Post-release arm travel is limited
- Arm decelerates rapidly after ball release

## Shortened Release

### Definition

A Shortened Release is characterized by a restricted bowling-arm release path in which the arm does not fully progress through its potential release range. The release action appears abbreviated, with reduced extension and limited movement continuation around the point of ball release.

### Identification Criteria
- Release Arm Range: Low
- Release Extension: Reduced
- Arm Path Length: Short
- Release Completion: Partial or Restricted
- Movement Continuation: Limited

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Shortened Release if:

- Full arm extension is achieved at release
- Release mechanics are complete and unrestricted
- Follow-through continues naturally after release
- Arm path length is substantial
- Release position reaches full functional range

# Alignment and sequencing 

## Side-On Position

### Definition

A Side-On Position is characterized by the hips and shoulders remaining predominantly aligned sideways relative to the target line. The bowler presents the side of the body toward the batter, creating a closed alignment pattern throughout the action.

### Identification Criteria
- Shoulder Alignment: Side-Oriented
- Hip Alignment: Side-Oriented
- Front-On Rotation: Minimal
- Body Presentation: Lateral
- Alignment Consistency: High

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Side-On Position if:

- The chest frequently faces the target
- Hips open substantially toward the batter
- Alignment remains predominantly front-on
- Cross-body positioning becomes dominant
- Shoulder orientation is largely open

## Front-On Position

### Definition

A Front-On Position is characterized by the hips and shoulders being predominantly oriented toward the target line. The bowler presents the front of the body toward the batter, creating an open alignment pattern throughout the action.

### Identification Criteria
- Shoulder Alignment: Open
- Hip Alignment: Open
- Target-Line Orientation: High
- Chest Presentation: Forward
- Body Openness: High

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Front-On Position if:

- The body remains predominantly side-facing
- Hips stay closed throughout the action
- Shoulder orientation remains lateral
- Side-on positioning dominates
- Cross-body alignment becomes the defining characteristic

## Cross-Body Sequence

### Definition

A Cross-Body Sequence is characterized by a noticeable separation and interaction between hip and shoulder movement during the bowling action. The hips and shoulders do not move as a single unit, creating a cross-body rotational pattern that contributes to sequencing and force transfer.

### Identification Criteria
- Hip-Shoulder Separation: High
- Segment Independence: High
- Rotational Differentiation: Present
- Cross-Body Motion Pattern: Evident
- Alignment Separation: Pronounced

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Cross-Body Sequence if:

- Hips and shoulders move together as a unit
- Alignment separation remains consistently low
- Rotational differentiation is minimal
- Segment independence is absent
- Body movement appears unified rather than sequenced

## Hip-Led Bowler

### Definition

A Hip-Led Bowler is characterized by movement initiation originating primarily from the hips. The lower trunk and pelvic region begin the sequencing process, with the shoulders and upper body subsequently following the movement created by the hips.

### Identification Criteria
- Hip Movement Initiation: Early
- Shoulder Response: Secondary
- Hip-Shoulder Sequencing: Hip First
- Rotational Leadership: Hip Dominant
- Movement Organization: Lower Segment Initiated

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Hip-Led Bowler if:

- Shoulder movement consistently precedes hip movement
- Upper-body rotation initiates the action
- Hip contribution is secondary
- Sequencing begins from the shoulders
- Movement organization is primarily upper-body driven

## Shoulder-Led Bowler

### Definition

A Shoulder-Led Bowler is characterized by movement initiation originating primarily from the shoulders and upper torso. Rotational and directional changes begin in the upper body, with the hips and lower body responding to the movement generated above.

### Identification Criteria
- Shoulder Movement Initiation: Early
- Hip Response: Secondary
- Hip-Shoulder Sequencing: Shoulders First
- Rotational Leadership: Shoulder Dominant
- Movement Organization: Upper Segment Initiated

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Shoulder-Led Bowler if:

- Hip movement consistently precedes shoulder movement
- Sequencing originates from the pelvis
- Lower-body initiation is dominant
- Shoulders primarily respond rather than lead
- Movement organization begins from the hips

## Lower-Body Driven

### Definition

A Lower-Body Driven bowler is characterized by force generation originating primarily from the legs, hips, and ground-contact mechanics. Momentum is built from the lower body and transferred upward through the kinetic chain into the trunk, shoulders, and bowling arm.

### Identification Criteria

- Leg Drive: High
- Hip Contribution: High
- Ground Force Utilization: High
- Momentum Transfer: Bottom-Up

### Exclusions

- Most momentum originates from the shoulders
- Upper-body rotation dominates force production
- Leg contribution is limited
- Ground-force utilization is minimal
- Lower-body involvement is secondary

## Upper-Body Driven

### Definition

An Upper-Body Driven bowler is characterized by force generation originating primarily from the trunk, shoulders, and bowling-arm mechanics. Momentum is created predominantly through upper-body rotation and arm acceleration rather than through substantial lower-body contribution.

### Identification Criteria
- Upper-Body Contribution: High
- Shoulder Contribution: High
- Trunk Rotation Contribution: High
- Arm Acceleration Contribution: High
- Momentum Transfer: Top-Down

### Exclusions

A bowler should generally not be classified as Upper-Body Driven if:

- Lower-body contribution dominates force generation
- Upper-body involvement is secondary
- Leg drive is the defining feature of the action

A Head-Controlled bowler is characterized by movement organization centered around stable and purposeful head positioning. The head acts as the primary reference point for balance, alignment, and directional control, with body segments adapting to maintain head stability throughout the action.
