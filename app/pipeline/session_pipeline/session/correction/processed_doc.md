# Cricket Bowling Biomechanics Correction Knowledge Base

## Types of phases : 

1. Gather Phase
2. Backfoot Phase
3. Delivery stride Phase
4. Frontfoot Phase
5. Follow through Phase

---

# Gather

## Pelvis tilts excessively during pre-delivery jump

### Definition

The bowler's hips tilt too much to one side during the pre-delivery jump, reducing balance and making it harder to maintain an efficient bowling action.

### Identification Criteria
- Hip loading remains consistently biased to one side during the gather phase.
- One hip stays noticeably higher than the other for most of the gather phase.
- Hip direction measurements show large or persistent pelvic tilt during the pre-delivery jump.

### Correction Cue:
Keep your hips level in the air by engaging your glutes and avoiding sideways drift.

### Impact
- Improves balance during the pre-delivery jump, helping the bowler stay stable and controlled.
- Creates a more consistent release point, leading to better accuracy.
- Allows more energy to be directed towards the target, improving bowling efficiency and reducing unnecessary stress on the body.

# Backfoot

## Weak back-leg push-off impulse

### Definition

The bowler does not push strongly enough off the back leg, reducing momentum and limiting pace generation.

### Identification Criteria
- The back knee remains excessively flexed throughout the backfoot phase, indicating poor force production from the back leg.
- The back knee shows limited extension during the backfoot phase, suggesting a weak push-off impulse.

### Correction Cue:
Push harder off the back leg by extending the hip explosively — imagine you’re jumping forward off a starting block.

### Impact
- Generates more forward momentum, helping the bowler bowl with greater pace.
- Improves energy transfer through the bowling action, making the action more efficient.
- Helps the bowler move smoothly into the delivery stride and maintain better rhythm.

# Frontfoot

## Hip–shoulder separation peaks too early

### Definition

The hips and shoulders start rotating too early, reducing the amount of power generated during ball release.

### Identification Criteria
- Hip–shoulder separation remains excessively high during the frontfoot phase, indicating that trunk rotation has started too early.
- Hip–shoulder alignment values decrease rapidly immediately after front foot contact (FFC), suggesting premature shoulder rotation.

### Correction Cue:
Delay your trunk rotation by keeping your shoulders closed for 2–3 frames longer after FFC; let the hips lead first, then the shoulders accelerate past them.

### Impact
- Improves power generation by allowing the hips and shoulders to work in sequence.
- Increases bowling pace by transferring more energy into the ball at release.
- Creates a smoother and more efficient bowling action, improving consistency.

## Pelvis rotates faster than the trunk

### Definition

The hips rotate much faster than the upper body, causing poor timing and reducing efficient transfer of power into the ball.

### Identification Criteria
- Hip–shoulder alignment remains excessively high during the frontfoot phase, indicating that the hips are rotating substantially ahead of the trunk.
- Hip–shoulder separation persists throughout the frontfoot phase, suggesting that the trunk is failing to accelerate and catch up with the hips.

### Correction Cue:
Activate stronger trunk acceleration by pulling the non-bowling arm down earlier and tightening your core so the shoulders ‘chase’ the hips.

### Impact
- Improves timing between the hips and shoulders, creating a smoother bowling action.
- Allows more power to be transferred into the ball, helping increase pace.
- Produces a more consistent release, improving control and accuracy.

## Front-leg bracing occurs after release

### Definition

The front leg becomes firm too late, after the ball has already been released, reducing power transfer and bowling efficiency.

### Identification Criteria
- The front foot knee remains flexed during the frontfoot phase instead of achieving a braced or straight position before release.
- The average front knee angle remains substantially below a braced/straight position at ball release.

### Correction Cue:
Land with a slightly bent knee and snap it straight before release — think of ‘hitting the wall’ with your front leg.

### Impact
- Improves power transfer through the body, helping the bowler generate more pace.
- Creates a stronger and more stable base at release, improving control and consistency.
- Makes the bowling action more efficient by allowing energy to move into the ball instead of being lost.

## Slow knee extension in front leg

### Definition

The front knee straightens too slowly after landing, reducing force transfer and limiting bowling power.

### Identification Criteria
- The front foot knee remains flexed throughout the frontfoot phase instead of extending rapidly into a braced position.
- The average front knee angle remains below the expected braced/straight position during ball release, indicating delayed knee extension.

### Correction Cue:
Snap the knee straight immediately on landing — imagine firing the leg into the ground.

### Impact
- Creates a stronger front-leg brace, helping the bowler generate more pace.
- Improves force transfer from the ground into the ball, making the action more efficient.
- Provides a more stable base at release, improving balance and consistency.

## Chest stays closed at release

### Definition

The chest does not rotate enough toward the batter at ball release, reducing power transfer and limiting ball speed.

### Identification Criteria
- Hip–shoulder alignment remains excessively high during the frontfoot phase, indicating incomplete upper-body rotation at release.
- Hip–shoulder separation persists until ball release, suggesting that the chest has not opened sufficiently toward the batter.

### Correction Cue:
Rotate your chest fully toward the batter during trunk flexion to lengthen the delivery arc.

### Impact
- Allows more power to be transferred into the ball, helping increase bowling pace.
- Produces a more complete and efficient bowling action, reducing wasted energy.
- Improves follow-through and release consistency, helping the bowler bowl more accurately.

## Delay between FFC and shoulder rotation

### Definition

The shoulders start rotating too late after the front foot lands, reducing the smooth transfer of power into the ball.

### Identification Criteria
- Hip–shoulder alignment remains excessively high throughout the frontfoot phase, indicating delayed shoulder rotation after front foot contact (FFC).
- Hip–shoulder separation persists at ball release, suggesting that the shoulders are rotating later than expected.

### Correction Cue:
Start rotating your trunk as soon as the front foot plants — the moment the heel hits, the shoulders must follow.

### Impact
- Improves the timing between the lower and upper body, creating a smoother bowling action.
- Transfers energy more efficiently into the ball, helping generate greater pace.
- Produces a more consistent release, improving control and accuracy.

## Arm speed high but trunk speed lagging

### Definition

The bowling arm moves quickly, but the trunk rotates too slowly, reducing efficient power transfer and limiting ball speed.

### Identification Criteria
- Hip–shoulder alignment remains excessively high during the frontfoot phase, indicating that the trunk is lagging behind the rotating arm.
- Hip–shoulder separation persists throughout the frontfoot phase, suggesting delayed trunk acceleration relative to the upper body.

### Correction Cue:
Accelerate your trunk earlier by engaging the obliques and serratus muscles during hip rotation.

### Impact
- Improves coordination between the trunk and bowling arm, creating a smoother action.
- Allows more power to be transferred into the ball, helping increase bowling pace.
- Reduces extra stress on the shoulder and arm by sharing the workload across the whole body.

# Follow through 

## Excessive lateral braking in follow-through

### Definition

The bowler slows down too sharply sideways during the follow-through, reducing balance and increasing stress on the body.

### Identification Criteria
- Balance post-delivery remains consistently poor, indicating abrupt sideways braking during the follow-through.
- Trunk lateral flexion values remain excessively high during the follow-through, suggesting excessive sideways deceleration instead of moving straight through the crease.

### Correction Cue:
Drive straight through the crease with your hips and keep the chest facing the target slightly longer.

### Impact
- Improves balance after release, helping the bowler finish the action in control.
- Allows momentum to continue toward the target, making the bowling action more efficient.
- Reduces unnecessary stress on the lower back and sides of the body, lowering injury risk.
