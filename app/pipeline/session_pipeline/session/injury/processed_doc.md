# Gather

## Back & Hip Stress

### Definition
Drifting sideways during the run-up places extra stress on the lower back and hips, increasing the risk of overuse injuries.

### Fault
Lateral drift

#### Definition 
Lateral drift occurs when the bowler moves sideways during the run-up or gather instead of moving straight toward the target.

### Identification Criteria
- Consistent sideways head lean or movement during the gather phase.
- One hip remains noticeably higher than the other for most of the gather phase.
- Large side-to-side variations in hip loading indicate the body is drifting rather than moving straight.
- Excessive changes in shoulder tilt suggest lateral movement instead of a stable, balanced gather.

### Feedback
When you drift sideways late, your body compensates later. Keep your last 5–6 steps straight into the crease.

## Lumbar Overload

### Definition
Slowing down or braking during the run-up places excessive stress on the lower back, increasing the risk of lumbar injuries.

### Fault
Deceleration

#### Definition
Deceleration occurs when the bowler slows down or brakes during the run-up or gather instead of maintaining smooth, continuous forward momentum.

### Identification Criteria
- Shoulder tilt progression shows abrupt changes or interruptions rather than smooth continuous motion.
- Movement rhythm during the gather phase appears jerky or inconsistent, indicating braking or slowing down.
- The bowler loses fluidity while transitioning into the delivery stride.

### Feedback
Stay smooth right through the crease — braking costs pace and loads your back.

## Back Compensation

### Definition
When the head drifts excessively to one side, the body compensates by overloading the lower back, increasing the risk of back injuries.

### Fault
Head drift

#### Definition
Head drift occurs when the bowler's head moves or leans excessively to one side instead of staying centered and stable during the bowling action.

### Identification Criteria

- The head shows a consistent lean to one side throughout the gather phase.
- Head stability measurements indicate excessive side-to-side movement during the gather.
- The bowler struggles to keep the head centered over the base of support while loading.

### Feedback
Keep your head stacked — accuracy and spinal safety improve instantly.

# Backfoot

## Spinal Load

### Definition
Jumping too high during the pre-delivery jump increases the forces on the spine, raising the risk of lower back injuries.

### Fault
Excess vertical jump

#### Definition
Excess vertical jump occurs when the bowler jumps too high during the pre-delivery jump instead of moving efficiently forward toward the crease.

### Identification Criteria
- The back knee remains excessively extended during back foot contact, indicating insufficient absorption of landing forces.
- The back knee shows limited flexion after landing, suggesting a stiff and overly vertical pre-delivery jump.
- The bowler maintains a tall, upright posture with minimal compression through the back leg during the backfoot phase.

### Feedback
Think forward, not upward. A flatter jump protects your back and keeps pace efficient.

## Counter-rotation

### Definition
A closed back foot forces the upper body to rotate against the hips, increasing stress on the lower back and raising the risk of injury.

### Fault
Closed BFC

#### Definition
Closed BFC occurs when the back foot lands in an excessively closed or side-on position during back foot contact, restricting natural body rotation.

### Identification Criteria
- The bowler adopts a closed back foot contact position during the backfoot phase.
- The back foot angle indicates an excessively side-on or closed alignment relative to the direction of delivery.
- The bowler stance metric classifies the action as excessively side-on, increasing the likelihood of counter-rotation.

### Feedback
That closed back foot twists your back — let it open slightly to rotate freely.

## Lumbar Injury

### Definition
A mixed bowling action causes the hips and shoulders to rotate in different directions, increasing stress on the lower back and raising the risk of lumbar injuries.

### Fault
Mixed action

#### Definition
Mixed action occurs when the hips and shoulders are misaligned, causing the body to combine front-on and side-on techniques during the bowling action.

### Identification Criteria
- The bowler stance is classified as a Mixed Action during the backfoot phase.
- The back foot alignment falls between a side-on and front-on position, indicating a mixed action pattern.

### Feedback
Let the hips go first, then the shoulders follow.

## Stress Fractures

### Definition
Excessive twisting between the hips and shoulders places high stress on the spine, increasing the risk of stress fractures in the lower back.

### Fault
Counter-rotation

#### Definition
Counter-rotation occurs when the shoulders rotate excessively against the hips during the bowling action, creating excessive twisting through the lower back.

### Identification Criteria

- The bowler adopts a strongly closed back foot contact position during the backfoot phase.
- The bowler stance is classified as excessively side-on, increasing the likelihood of counter-rotation.
- The hip loading pattern remains strongly biased to one side during back foot contact, suggesting restricted pelvic rotation.

### Feedback
Twisting against yourself is the biggest red flag — we'll clean that up.


# Delivery Stride

## Lumbar Injury

### Definition
A mixed bowling action causes the hips and shoulders to rotate in different directions, increasing stress on the lower back and raising the risk of lumbar injuries.

### Fault
Mixed action

#### Definition
Mixed action occurs when the hips and shoulders rotate in different directions or are poorly synchronized during the bowling action.

### Identification Criteria

- Hip–shoulder alignment shows excessive separation or inconsistent alignment during the delivery stride.
- The hips and shoulders rotate in different directions or are poorly synchronized during the delivery stride.
- Hip–shoulder alignment values remain persistently high, indicating a mixed action pattern rather than coordinated body rotation.

### Feedback
Let the hips go first, then the shoulders follow.

## Knee Stress

### Definition
A crossing delivery stride places excessive sideways forces on the front knee, increasing the risk of knee injuries.

### Fault
Crossing stride

#### Definition
Crossing stride occurs when the front foot lands across the body's midline instead of landing straight toward the target during the delivery stride.

### Identification Criteria
- Delivery stride direction deviates across the body instead of remaining straight toward the target.
- The stride direction angle indicates a cross-over landing pattern during the delivery stride.

### Feedback
Land straight under your body to stay strong through the front leg.

## Front Knee & Back Stress

### Definition
Taking an excessively long stride places extra stress on the front knee and lower back, increasing the risk of injury.

### Fault
Overstride

### Identification Criteria
- Delivery stride length is excessively long relative to the bowler's normal stride pattern.
- The stride length metric consistently indicates an overstriding pattern during the delivery stride.

#### Definition
Overstride occurs when the bowler takes an excessively long delivery stride, causing the front foot to land too far ahead of the body.

### Feedback
Overstriding feels powerful but overloads your knee — let's shorten it slightly.

## Stress Fractures

### Definition
Excessive sideways leaning of the trunk places high stress on the spine, increasing the risk of stress fractures in the lower back.

### Fault
Side lean

#### Definition
Side lean occurs when the bowler bends excessively to one side during the delivery stride instead of staying upright and balanced.

### Identification Criteria
- Trunk lateral flexion values remain consistently high during the delivery stride.
- The bowler exhibits excessive sideways trunk lean (falling away) during the delivery stride.

### Feedback
Falling away puts side-load on your spine — finish taller.

## Back Compensation

### Definition
When the head drifts excessively to one side during delivery, the body compensates by overloading the lower back, increasing the risk of back injuries.

### Fault
Head drift

#### Definition
Head drift occurs when the bowler's head moves or leans excessively to one side during the delivery stride instead of staying centered over the body.

### Identification Criteria
- Head position shows a consistent lean or drift to one side during the delivery stride.
- Head stability measurements indicate excessive side-to-side movement during the delivery stride.

### Feedback
Keep your head stacked — accuracy and spinal safety improve instantly.


# Frontfoot

## Lumbar Stress

### Definition
When the front leg collapses after landing, the lower back absorbs extra forces, increasing the risk of lumbar injuries.

### Fault
Collapse

#### Definition
Collapse occurs when the front leg bends excessively after front foot contact instead of remaining firm and braced through ball release.

### Identification Criteria
- The front foot knee bracing metric classifies the front leg as flexed or collapsing rather than braced.
- The average front knee angle at front foot contact remains substantially below a braced/straight position.

### Feedback
Brace the front leg before release — that gives pace without extra effort.

## Shoulder Stress

### Definition
When the hips rotate too late, the shoulders must generate extra force, increasing stress on the shoulder and raising the risk of injury.

### Fault
Late hips

#### Definition
Late hips occur when the hips rotate too slowly or too late during the frontfoot phase, forcing the upper body and shoulders to generate more of the bowling force.

### Identification Criteria
- Hip–shoulder alignment remains excessively high at front foot contact, indicating delayed hip rotation.
- Hip–shoulder separation stays persistently large during the frontfoot phase, suggesting the hips are rotating later than the upper body.

### Feedback
Fire the hips first — it takes pressure off your shoulder.

## Disc Load

### Definition
Excessive trunk bending places high pressure on the spinal discs, increasing the risk of lower back injuries.

### Fault
Excess flexion

#### Definition
Excess flexion occurs when the bowler bends the trunk excessively during ball release instead of maintaining a strong and stable posture.

### Identification Criteria
- Trunk lateral flexion values remain excessively high during the frontfoot phase.
- The bowler exhibits excessive trunk bend during ball release.

### Feedback
Stay tall longer; bend forward only as you release.

## Stress Fractures

### Definition
Excessive sideways leaning of the trunk places high stress on the spine, increasing the risk of stress fractures in the lower back.

### Fault
Side lean

#### Definition
Side lean occurs when the bowler bends excessively to one side during ball release instead of staying upright and balanced.

### Identification Criteria
- Trunk lateral flexion values remain consistently high during the frontfoot phase.
- The bowler exhibits excessive sideways trunk lean during ball release.

### Feedback
Falling away puts side-load on your spine — finish taller.


# Follow through

## Lumbar Compression

### Definition
Falling away during the follow-through places excessive compressive forces on the lower back, increasing the risk of lumbar injuries.

### Fault
Falling away

#### Definition
Falling away occurs when the bowler's body leans excessively away from the target during the follow-through instead of finishing in a straight, balanced position.

### Identification Criteria
- Trunk lateral flexion values remain consistently high during the follow-through phase.
- The bowler exhibits excessive sideways trunk lean or falls away from the target during follow-through

### Feedback
Finish straight — let the forces run out safely.

## Spine & Knee Injury

### Definition
Stopping the body too abruptly during the follow-through places excessive stress on the spine and knees, increasing the risk of injury.

### Fault
Abrupt stop

#### Definition
Abrupt stop occurs when the bowler slows down or stops suddenly during the follow-through instead of allowing the body to decelerate smoothly.

### Identification Criteria
- Balance post-delivery remains consistently poor, with a low percentage of balanced frames during the follow-through.
- The bowler is unable to maintain body balance after ball release, indicating an abrupt deceleration pattern.

### Feedback
Don't block the action — let your body slow naturally.
